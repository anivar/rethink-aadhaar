question: "Isn't Aadhaar just like the US Social Security Number?"
short: "Aadhaar is not comparable to the SSN. They are very different things."
---

The Social Security Number (SSN), the nine‑digit number widely used by US government agencies, is often cited as evidence that "advanced economies" do something similar to India's unique identity project. But there are important differences between the two, starting with the fact that the SSN is, well, not an identity number.

The SSN was created to track contributions to and payouts from a single specific welfare programme: Social Security. It carries no biometrics. It is not centralised across every database. Possession of the SSN does not require a person to surrender fingerprints, iris scans, or a photograph to a single state agency. And US courts have repeatedly limited the contexts in which an SSN can be demanded.

Aadhaar, by contrast, is a biometric identity number that has been seeded into every conceivable government database and many private ones, with mandatory authentication at the point of use. The footprint keeps widening: Apaar IDs for school students, Aadhaar‑seeded voter rolls, ABHA health IDs, the Account Aggregator framework. Each rollout extends the same architecture into a fresh domain of life.
