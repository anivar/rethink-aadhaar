> *Despite a mountain of evidence on Aadhaar's failure, "Aadhaar evangelism" — efforts to convert other countries to the Aadhaar biometric identity model — is spreading across the world. Rethink Aadhaar campaigned to caution other countries and share our concerns. The statement below, endorsed by more than 50 Indian organisations aside from many individual signatories, was released on 10 December 2025 (Human Rights Day).*

## Beware of Aadhaar

### A Warning on India's Biometric Identity Model

We, concerned Indian citizens and organisations, are alarmed to note that efforts are being made to promote biometric identity systems similar to Aadhaar in other countries.

Aadhaar is India's unique identity number, linked with a person's biometrics — fingerprints, iris and photograph as of now. Rolled out from 2009 onwards in close collaboration with the IT industry, the use of Aadhaar and of Aadhaar‑based biometric authentication (ABBA) was promoted to the hilt by the Indian government. Aadhaar was supposed to be voluntary, but it quickly became clear that living without it would be very difficult. Today, it is as good as compulsory. Most social benefits are out of reach without Aadhaar.

For our part, we view Aadhaar as a failed and objectionable model that should not be replicated in other countries.

### Our main concerns

1. **A centralised database of biometrics and demographics** is a dangerous tool of social control — especially in the hands of an authoritarian government.

2. **The linkage of numerous databases with Aadhaar** magnifies the danger of it becoming a tool for profiling, surveillance, exclusion and worse. Centralised databases also pose data‑security risks by creating a single point of failure.

3. **The "non-core" Aadhaar data is freely shared** with authorised users of authentication, with minimal safeguards — a major infringement of privacy.

4. **The demographic details are full of errors** from a hasty rollout. Severe restrictions on correction force people to align other documents with unreliable data, causing endless hassle to the poor and excluding many from social benefits.

5. **Biometric failures** are another major source of social exclusion, especially for the elderly. Aadhaar was rolled out without any transparency about the reliability of biometric authentication.

6. **A significant minority of people**, mainly from marginalised groups including disabled persons, do not have Aadhaar through no fault of their own — and are excluded from most social benefits.

7. **A lost Aadhaar number can be very difficult to retrieve.** Poor people have been forced to make long and expensive trips to regional assistance centres; some never managed to retrieve theirs and are now deprived of all social benefits.

8. **The coercive "seeding"** of Aadhaar with endless databases (ration cards, job cards, pension lists, bank accounts, voter lists) is a monumental waste of time for both functionaries and citizens.

9. **The rush for correction or update** has led to humongous queues at many centres, where people often wait in line for hours with no guarantee of remedy. There are no facilities for tracking grievance redressal, continuity of assistance, or other relief.

10. **Far from rooting out corruption**, Aadhaar's centralised database has enhanced information asymmetries and reduced transparency. Integration with the banking system has magnified exposure to identity fraud.

11. **At every step, Aadhaar has been a law unto itself.** It began without any legal backing. The Aadhaar Act was passed by bypassing the Upper House of Parliament. UIDAI often violates Supreme Court orders. A critical provision for parliamentary oversight was dropped in the final version of the Act.

The promoters of Aadhaar were never able to justify this particular identity model, or to explain what ills it is supposed to remedy. Many countries have functional identity systems that are less coercive, invasive, exclusionary and unreliable than Aadhaar.

We urge the greatest caution from countries that are considering replication of the Aadhaar model. We would be happy to facilitate field visits for anyone interested in understanding these problems in more detail.
